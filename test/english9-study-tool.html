Literary Terms — 23 to Know
ImageryLanguage appealing to the senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch). Night example: smoke and flames from the crematoria.
ThemeThe central message or lesson of a text.
ToneThe AUTHOR'S attitude toward the subject. Wiesel's tone: somber, reflective. Tone = writer's feeling.
MoodThe feeling experienced by the READER. Night's mood: grief, dread, despair. Mood = reader's feeling.
SimileComparison using "like" or "as." Example: "Her eyes shone like stars."
MetaphorDirect comparison WITHOUT "like" or "as." R&J example: "Juliet is the sun."
PersonificationGiving human qualities to something nonhuman. Example: "The night whispered their secrets."
ForeshadowingHints or clues about what will happen later. R&J: "These violent delights have violent ends."
FlashbackA scene that interrupts the present to show something from the past.
SoliloquyCharacter speaks thoughts aloud, often alone on stage. Reveals inner feelings. Used in R&J.
Dramatic IronyAUDIENCE knows something CHARACTERS don't. R&J: We know Juliet is alive; Romeo doesn't.
NarrativeA story or account of events.
MemoirNONFICTION account based on personal experience and memory. Night is a memoir.
StereotypeAn oversimplified belief about a group of people.
OxymoronTwo contradictory words together. Example: "sweet sorrow" (R&J).
Symbol vs. MotifSymbol = one image representing a bigger idea. Motif = a REPEATED pattern across the whole text. "Night" is a motif; the corpse in the mirror is a symbol.
AllegoryA story with both a literal meaning AND a deeper symbolic meaning.
Internal vs. External ConflictInternal = struggle inside a character's mind. External = struggle against a person, society, or nature.
Protagonist vs. AntagonistProtagonist = central character. Antagonist = opposing character or force.
Denotation vs. ConnotationDenotation = literal dictionary meaning. Connotation = emotional/cultural association beyond the definition.
CharacterizationHow an author reveals personality: actions, words, thoughts, appearance, others' reactions.
SubtextHidden or underlying meaning beneath what a character says. Key in Act 4 of R&J (Juliet and Paris scene).
Iambic Pentameter10 syllables per line, alternating unstressed/stressed: da-DUM × 5. Used in Romeo and Juliet.
Parts of Plot1. Exposition → 2. Rising Action → 3. Climax → 4. Falling Action → 5. Resolution

Romeo & Juliet — Act by Act
Act 1 OverviewFamily feud established. Romeo lovesick over Rosaline. Capulet party. Romeo and Juliet meet and fall in love. Both horrified to learn they are enemies.
RomeoMontague; emotional, impulsive; first loves Rosaline, then Juliet
JulietCapulet; 13, almost 14 years old; cautious and thoughtful in the balcony scene; grows bolder as the play progresses
MercutioRomeo's close friend; hotheaded, witty; neither Montague nor Capulet; killed by Tybalt in Act 3
TybaltJuliet's cousin; Capulet; aggressive and violent; kills Mercutio; killed by Romeo
Friar LawrenceMarries Romeo and Juliet; gives Juliet the potion; sends the critical letter that never arrives
Act 2 — Balcony SceneJuliet is cautious: "If thy bent of love be honorable, thy purpose marriage…" They plan to marry in secret. Dramatic irony: audience knows their families are enemies.
Why Friar agreesHe hopes the marriage will end the feud between the families — a peace motive, not just love
"These violent delights…"Friar Lawrence warns Romeo that intense passion leads to destruction — foreshadowing of the ending
Act 3 — ClimaxRomeo refuses to fight Tybalt (loves him as Juliet's cousin). Mercutio fights Tybalt and is killed. Romeo kills Tybalt. Romeo is BANISHED from Verona.
Night/Day symbolismNight = safety and love for Romeo and Juliet. Day = danger and violence from the feud.
Act 4 — Friar's PlanJuliet drinks potion → appears dead. Family places her in the tomb. Friar sends letter to Romeo. Romeo is to come rescue her.
Act 4 — SubtextJuliet speaks to Paris with hidden meaning — she plans to fake death, not accept the marriage. Her words have double meaning.
Act 5 — The Chain of FailureFriar John quarantined → letter never reaches Romeo. Balthasar tells Romeo "Juliet is dead." Romeo buys poison. Rushes to tomb. Kills Paris. Dies next to Juliet. She wakes, sees Romeo dead, takes his dagger. Both dead. Feud ends.
Key Quotes"What's in a name?" (identity); "These violent delights have violent ends" (foreshadowing); "A plague o' both your houses!" (Mercutio dying); "O happy dagger!" (irony)
Dramatic Irony (Acts 4–5)WE know Juliet is alive. Romeo does NOT. He kills himself believing she is dead. This gap = the tragedy.
Fate vs. Free WillBoth at work: "star-crossed" fate + Romeo's impulsive choices + Friar's failed plan + chance quarantine = tragedy
Shakespeare's languageEarly Modern English. Iambic pentameter for noble characters. Prose for common characters.
Cross-Text Themes — Both Night & R&J
Family SeparationNight: Elie separated from mother, sister; loses father. R&J: Romeo and Juliet separated by family hatred.
Failed CommunicationNight: Moishe and Mrs. Schächter's warnings ignored. R&J: Friar's letter never reaches Romeo.
Loss of InnocenceNight: Elie loses faith, identity, childhood. R&J: Young love destroyed by adult conflict and hatred.
Choices and ConsequencesNight: survival choices forced by cruelty. R&J: impulsive choices (Romeo killing Tybalt) lead directly to tragedy.
Death and IdentityNight: Elie sees a corpse in the mirror — his identity is gone. R&J: the lovers' identities destroyed by the feud.
Essay Thesis IdeaBoth texts show that hatred — whether systemic (the Holocaust) or social (a family feud) — creates conditions where tragedy destroys the innocent along with the guilty.
Essay Outlines & Writing Tips

Main Essay — Night + R&J (Hatred, Communication, Choices)

1. INTRO: Name both texts + authors. Give context. Thesis: "Both Night and Romeo and Juliet show how hatred, silence, and impulsive choices lead to devastating consequences for innocent people."
2. NIGHT BODY: Ignored warnings (Moishe, Mrs. Schächter) → dehumanization (bread scene, cattle cars) → loss of faith ("Never shall I forget") → family separation → mirror passage (spiritual death)
3. R&J BODY: Family feud as root cause → secrecy and its consequences → Romeo's impulsive choice to kill Tybalt → failed letter (Friar John quarantined) → both lovers die
4. CONCLUSION: Restate thesis. Larger lesson: indifference and hatred do not stay contained — they destroy the innocent. Connect both texts to human responsibility.

Strong Analytical Verbs — Use These, Not "Shows" or "Proves"

✓ reveals   suggests   emphasizes   symbolizes   foreshadows   demonstrates   reinforces   contrasts   illustrates   underscores
✗ DO NOT just retell the plot. After every sentence ask: "What does this SHOW about theme or character?"

Night Symbolism — 3 to Know Cold

1. "Night"/darkness = suffering, silence of God, loss of innocence — appears at every major moment of horror
2. "Never shall I forget" = Elie's faith permanently broken; structured as an anti-prayer
3. Final mirror = corpse = spiritual death; "he/his" not "I/my" = psychological separation from former self

R&J Fate vs. Free Will — Evidence for Both

Fate: "star-crossed lovers" (Prologue); Friar John quarantined by accident; timing always slightly off
Free: Romeo kills Tybalt (Act 3); buys poison without confirming news (Act 5); Juliet takes dagger; Friar's risky plan
Both: Unjust system (feud) + impulsive choices + bad luck = inevitable tragedy. Shakespeare's warning.

MLA Essay Checklist

✓ Introduction with a clear thesis statement
✓ Text evidence — specific quotes or details from the texts
✓ Explanation of each quote — WHY does it matter for the theme?
✓ Parenthetical citations if required
✓ Analysis, not summary — what does the evidence SHOW?
✓ Conclusion that restates thesis and explains the larger lesson
