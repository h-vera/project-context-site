ğŸ¤ Who Is the Son of Man? Corporate and Individual

Later in Daniel 7, the interpretation reveals that "the saints of the Most High" receive the kingdom (7:18, 22, 27). This creates a fruitful tension: is the "son of man" an individual figure or a symbol for God's faithful people?

ğŸ‘¥ Corporate Reading

  • The "son of man" represents "the saints of the Most High" â€” faithful Israel
  • Just as each beast represents a kingdom/people, the human figure represents God's people
  • The kingdom is given to the community of the faithful

ğŸ‘¤ Individual Reading

  • The figure is distinguished from the saints â€” he comes on clouds and approaches God's throne
  • He receives worship (7:14) â€” appropriate only for a divine or messianic figure
  • Second Temple texts increasingly read this as an individual heavenly figure
Key Insight: The answer is "both." In biblical thinking, a king represents and embodies his people. Jesus will claim to be the Son of Man (individual) while also inviting his followers to share in his kingdom (corporate). The one and the many belong together.

âœï¸ Jesus and the Son of Man

Jesus chose "Son of Man" as his primary way of referring to himself â€” over 80 times in the Gospels. This was not a common messianic title in Second Temple Judaism, which makes Jesus' choice all the more striking.

Why "Son of Man"?

  • Veiled yet revealing: The title allowed Jesus to hint at his identity without triggering immediate political alarm
  • Suffering + glory: Jesus fused Daniel 7 (glory) with Isaiah 53 (suffering) â€” the Son of Man "must suffer" before he is vindicated
  • True humanity: By calling himself "son of man," Jesus claims to be the truly human one â€” what Adam and Israel failed to be
  • Divine prerogative: Riding on clouds, receiving worship, judging the nations â€” these are things only God does
