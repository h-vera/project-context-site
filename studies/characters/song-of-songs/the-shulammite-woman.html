Summary: The Shulammite is the only explicitly named woman in the Song of Songs, appearing at crucial moments in the poem's structure. Her name connects her both to a specific location (possibly Shunem) and symbolically to Solomon through wordplay. She represents the ideal of covenant love that cannot be bought or coerced, embodying wisdom's teaching that true love transcends material wealth and power.

Theological Significance: The Shulammite's rejection of Solomon's wealth (8:10-12) establishes the Song's central theological claim: love is a divine flame that cannot be purchased or possessed through power. Her character demonstrates that authentic love operates outside transactional relationships, pointing toward the gratuitous nature of divine love.

Narrative Journey

The Call to Return (Song 6:13): The daughters of Jerusalem call out "Return, return, O Shulammite" four times, emphasizing both her importance and her elusive nature. This marks her first explicit naming, occurring at the poem's structural center.
The Dance of Mahanaim (Song 7:1): She is compared to "the dance of two camps," suggesting both her beauty in movement and possibly alluding to divine-human encounter (as at Mahanaim in Genesis 32:2).
Declaration of Belonging (Song 7:10): Following her naming, she declares "I am my beloved's, and his desire is for me," showing mutual possession and desire rather than one-sided ownership.
Rejection of Solomon's Vineyard (Song 8:11-12): In the climactic scene, she contrasts Solomon's vineyard worth a thousand pieces of silver with her own vineyard, which she keeps for herself and her beloved, establishing love's pricelessness.
Pattern Recognition: The Shulammite appears most prominently when the poem addresses questions of identity, possession, and the nature of true love versus transactional relationships.

Personal

Study Questions

  1. How does the Shulammite's name and its wordplay with Solomon contribute to the Song's message about love?
  2. What can we learn from her rejection of Solomon's wealth about the nature of true love?
  3. How does her declaration "I am my beloved's" differ from typical ancient Near Eastern views of marriage?
  4. What does the repeated call to "return" (6:13) suggest about the nature of love's pursuit?
  5. How does the Shulammite embody wisdom literature's teaching about relationships?
  6. In what ways does her story restore the Genesis 2 vision of partnership?
  7. How might her message have challenged both ancient and modern assumptions about women and marriage?
  8. What aspects of the Shulammite's love point us toward Christ's love for the Church?
