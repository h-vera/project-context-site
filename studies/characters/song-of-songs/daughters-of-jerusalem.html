Summary: The Daughters of Jerusalem function as a Greek chorus in the Song of Songs, providing communal witness to the love between the couple. They receive the beloved's confidences, ask questions that advance the dialogue, and are repeatedly charged with the wisdom refrain about not awakening love prematurely. Their presence situates private love within public community, suggesting that authentic love requires both intimate expression and social recognition.

Theological Significance: The Daughters of Jerusalem represent the necessary communal dimension of covenant love. Their repeated charge "not to stir up or awaken love until it pleases" establishes love's divine timing and the wisdom of patient waiting, while their witness function prefigures the Church as witness to Christ's love.

Narrative Journey

First Address (Song 1:5): The beloved addresses them about her appearance: "I am black but beautiful, O daughters of Jerusalem," establishing them as her confidantes and potential critics she must address.
First Charge (Song 2:7): The beloved charges them: "I adjure you, O daughters of Jerusalem, by the gazelles or the does of the field, that you not stir up or awaken love until it pleases." This refrain becomes their signature.
Wedding Procession (Song 3:10-11): They are called to "go out and look upon King Solomon" and his wedding procession, functioning as witnesses to public celebration.
Second Charge (Song 3:5): After finding her beloved, she repeats the charge not to awaken love, reinforcing the theme of divine timing.
Urgent Appeal (Song 5:8): The beloved charges them: "If you find my beloved, tell him I am sick with love," making them messengers in her search.
Dialogue Exchange (Song 5:9; 6:1): They ask "What is your beloved more than another beloved?" prompting her famous description, then "Where has your beloved gone?" advancing the narrative.
Final Charge (Song 8:4): The refrain appears one last time, modified slightly, concluding their role as guardians of love's timing.
Pattern Recognition: The Daughters appear at crucial transitions, marking movements between private intimacy and public recognition, always associated with the wisdom of waiting and witnessing.

Study Questions

  2. How do the Daughters of Jerusalem balance supporting and questioning the beloved?
  3. What role does community play in validating and protecting intimate relationships?
  4. How does their urban identity contrast with the pastoral setting of much of the Song?
