    Social Context: Barrenness in Ancient Israel

    Cultural Shame: Barrenness brought profound social stigma. Children were economic necessity (labor, old-age care), sign of divine favor, means of preserving family name. Barren women faced mockery, marginalization, questions about divine disfavor.

    Legal Vulnerability: Though not grounds for divorce in Israel (unlike some ANE codes), barrenness often led husbands to take additional wives/concubinesâ€”creating Peninnah-Hannah dynamic.

    Theological Questions: Barrenness raised questions: Why has God withheld blessing? Is this punishment? Has the woman sinned? Peninnah exploits these painful theological anxieties in her provocations.

    Worship Context: Annual pilgrimages to Shiloh emphasized family and children (Deut 16:11-14)â€”making Hannah's barrenness most visible, painful at worship festivals. Peninnah's timing maximizes humiliation in sacred space.
