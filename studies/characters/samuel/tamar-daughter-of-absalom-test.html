Tamar, Daughter of Absalom תָּמָר

Royal Daughter | Memorial Name | Genealogical Bridge
Profile Depth:
Minor Character: Brief Mentions

Overview

Scripture: 2 Samuel 14:27; 2 Chronicles 11:18-21 (as Maacah)
Hebrew: תָּמָר (Tāmār) "Palm Tree"
Etymology: From תמר = "palm tree," symbol of beauty, righteousness, and fruitfulness
Role: Absalom's only daughter; memorial to violated aunt; enters Judah's royal line
Setting: Post-rebellion period (c. 970 BCE); transition to divided monarchy

Tags: Royal Daughter Memorial Name Dynasty Continuity Genealogical Bridge Beauty Noted

Summary: Tamar, the only daughter of Absalom mentioned in Scripture, bears the name of her tragic aunt who was violated by Amnon. Born after Absalom's return from exile, she represents both memorial and hope—remembering injustice while embodying beauty and continuity. Chronicles suggests she (possibly under the name Maacah) enters the legitimate Davidic line through marriage, bridging the rebellious Absalom's line back into Judah's dynasty.

Theological Significance: This Tamar transforms a name of tragedy into one of restoration. While her aunt Tamar lived "desolate," this Tamar is described as "beautiful" and fruitful, entering the royal genealogy. Her story demonstrates how God can redeem painful family histories, using memorial names to both honor the violated and point toward restoration.

Narrative Journey

Birth as Memorial (2 Sam 14:27): Born to Absalom after his return from three-year exile in Geshur. Absalom names his only recorded daughter after his violated sister, making her a living memorial to family tragedy and his quest for justice.
Beauty Emphasized (2 Sam 14:27): Described as "a beautiful woman" (אִשָּׁה יְפַת מַרְאֶה), the same phrase used for her namesake aunt. This beauty, however, leads not to tragedy but to honor—redemptive reversal of her aunt's story.
Three Brothers (2 Sam 14:27): Has three brothers who remain unnamed and apparently die young (no record of their survival). Tamar alone carries Absalom's legacy forward, inverting typical patriarchal expectations.
Possible Marriage into Royal Line (2 Chr 11:20-21): If identified with Maacah, she marries Rehoboam and becomes his most beloved wife, mother of Abijah (next king of Judah). This would mean the rebel Absalom's line rejoins legitimate Davidic succession through a woman.
Pattern Recognition: Tamar's trajectory reverses her aunt's: from beauty to honor rather than violation; from memorial of tragedy to bridge of restoration; from desolation to dynasty. She embodies redemption of traumatic family history.

Position in Book

Mentioned during Absalom's waiting period before rebellion—between return from exile and uprising. Her birth represents attempted normalcy before chaos.

Literary Patterns

Character Function

Living memorial; genealogical bridge; beauty redeemed; name transformer. Silent presence speaking through nomenclature.

Narrative Techniques

Minimal detail maximizes significance. Name carries narrative weight. Chronicles connection expands meaning retrospectively.

Ancient Near Eastern Context & Biblical Distinctives

ANE Parallels

  • Memorial Names: Common practice to name children after deceased relatives
  • Royal Daughters: Used for political alliances and dynastic continuity
  • Beauty Noted: Physical appearance recorded for marriageable women
  • Throne Names: Royalty often took new names (Maacah possibly throne name)

Biblical Distinctives

  • Trauma Remembered: Name preserves memory of injustice, not just honor
  • Female Agency: Woman bridges hostile dynastic branches
  • Beauty Protected: Unlike ANE literature, her beauty leads to honor
  • Redemptive Genealogy: Focus on restoration, not just succession

Textual Clarification

Distinguishing the Tamars: This profile concerns Tamar, daughter of Absalom (2 Sam 14:27), not to be confused with:

  • Tamar, daughter of David (2 Sam 13) - Absalom's sister who was violated
  • Tamar in Genesis 38 - Judah's daughter-in-law
  • The erroneous tradition of a "Tamar daughter of Solomon" (not in Hebrew text)

The Maacah Connection: 2 Chronicles 11:20-21 mentions "Maacah daughter of Absalom" as Rehoboam's favored wife. Many scholars identify this Maacah with Tamar, suggesting either a throne name or textual variation. This would mean Absalom's daughter entered the legitimate Davidic succession.
