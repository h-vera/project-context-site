When God sees a human on a high mountain, surrendering and giving back to God what God gave in the first place, it brings a calmness upon Yahweh. This is the inverse of Genesis 6, where God looked at creation and saw only violence.

"I can work with a human who will surrender."

What Is a Covenant?

A berit is a formal commitment where two parties place themselves under obligations toward each other, usually with a ritual symbol (here, the rainbow) and stated benefits. But notice something striking: the word "covenant" never appears in the Eden narrative. Why not?

"Covenant is like duct tape."
It's good, but it's not best. It's the repair mechanism‚Äîwhat you need when trust has been broken. In Eden, there was no need to formalize the relationship. The covenant exists because something has gone wrong. As Jeremiah will promise about the new covenant: "My people won't need anyone to teach them the commands... the Torah will be written on their hearts" (Jer 31:33-34).

Genesis 9:1-7 replays the blessing of Genesis 1‚Äî"be fruitful and multiply"‚Äîbut with significant changes. God is accommodating His ideals to make room for human evil. Not to endorse it, but because that's the humanity He has to work with.

Eden Ideal (Gen 1:29-30)

Vegan diet for all. "Every plant with seed shall be food for you, and for every beast and bird." No creature has to exist at the expense of the life of another.

Post-Flood Reality (Gen 9:3)

"Every creeping thing that lives shall be food for you." God permits eating animals because humans have shown themselves to be violent‚Äîthey're going to kill anyway.

Eden Ideal (Gen 1:28)

Humans rule over animals in harmony. The relationship is one of stewardship and care, not domination.

Post-Flood Reality (Gen 9:2)

"The fear and terror of you will be upon every living creature." This is God naming a sad new reality, not assigning what He wanted. The animals will now run from violent humans.

ü©∏ Blood Is Life

"However, flesh with its life‚Äîthat is, its blood‚Äîyou shall not eat" (Gen 9:4).

God allows borrowing the flesh but not the blood. Why? The blood is the creature's life, and life belongs to the One who created it. Every time you kill an animal and withhold the blood, you're symbolically acknowledging: "This life is not mine. I'm borrowing it. The real life belongs to God."

This is the foundation of the Jewish kosher laws‚Äînot arbitrary rules, but a worldview that trades heavily in symbolism. Every meal becomes a reminder that humans don't create life; we receive it and steward it.

The Image of God and Bloodshed

"Whoever sheds the blood of a human, by a human shall their blood be shed, for in the image of God he made humanity."

‚Äî Genesis 9:6

If the life of an animal belongs to God, how much more the life of a human‚Äîmade in God's image? This is a direct response to Cain's murder and Lamech's violence. But note: this is a divine concession, not a divine ideal. God didn't execute Cain; He protected him. The stringent rule here stems from the fact that violence had become cosmic. God doesn't want another flood.

Important Context: Whatever view one holds on capital punishment, this verse should not be taken out of context as God's ultimate ideal. It's a concession to human evil, a guardrail against the escalation that led to the flood. The narrative placement matters.

The Hebrew word for rainbow is simply "bow"‚Äîthe same word for a war bow. God is hanging up His weapon. When the storm clouds gather and you might fear another flood, you'll see God's bow‚Äîpointed away from earth, a sign of peace.

"When the bow is in the cloud, I will see it and remember the everlasting covenant between God and every living creature of all flesh that is on the earth."

‚Äî Genesis 9:16

Notice: the rainbow is for God to see. "I will see it and remember." The sign isn't primarily for humans‚Äîit's God's own reminder of His commitment. When it appears that chaos is returning (storm clouds, rain), the bow appears as God's pledge that He will never again let de-creation consume the earth.

"God Remembered"

The phrase "God remembered" (Gen 8:1) doesn't mean God forgot. In Hebrew, zakar marks the moment when God acts on what He promised. From the human perspective, waiting feels like being forgotten. But "remembering" is the pivot point‚Äîthe moment everything changes.

This pattern repeats throughout Scripture: Sodom and Gomorrah ("God remembered Abraham," Gen 19:29), the Exodus ("God remembered his covenant," Ex 2:24), Hannah's barrenness ("Yahweh remembered her," 1 Sam 1:19). Each time, "God remembered" signals that deliverance is coming.

üîÑ The Cycle Begins Again

The covenant is established. The blessing is given. "Be fruitful and multiply." This is going to go great, right?

Immediately after, we're told: "Noah began to be a man of the ground, and he planted a vineyard" (Gen 9:20). A garden. Eating of the fruit. Becoming naked. The vocabulary of Genesis 3 returns in just two verses.

The Pattern: Garden folly (Noah) ‚Üí Son's crime (Ham) ‚Üí Grandson magnifies (Canaan/Nimrod) ‚Üí Cosmic rebellion (Babel) ‚Üí Divine judgment (scattering). The same melody plays again, teaching us what to expect as the story continues into Abraham and beyond.

The flood hasn't solved the problem of the human heart. But it has established something crucial: God will keep working with humanity. He will keep the cosmos stable. He will keep looking for humans who will surrender. And through one particular family‚Äîthe line of Shem‚ÄîHe will bring the ultimate Seed who will crush the serpent's head.
