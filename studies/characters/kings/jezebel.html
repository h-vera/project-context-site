Summary: Jezebel, daughter of King Ethbaal of Sidon, married Ahab to seal a political alliance but brought catastrophic religious and moral corruption to Israel. She promoted Baal worship, persecuted Yahweh's prophets, and orchestrated judicial murder. Her violent end fulfilled Elijah's prophecy, making her an enduring archetype of covenant betrayal and abuse of power.

Theological Significance: Jezebel personifies the twin evils of idolatry and injustice‚Äîthe misuse of royal power against God and the vulnerable. Her downfall vindicates prophetic truth and demonstrates divine justice against those who corrupt covenant faithfulness.

Narrative Journey

Political Alliance & Religious Revolution (1 Kings 16:31‚Äì34): Jezebel enters Israel's story through marriage to Ahab, bringing with her the worship of Baal and Asherah. This marks a watershed moment in Israel's apostasy, as institutional paganism replaces covenant faith.
Prophetic Confrontation (1 Kings 18‚Äì19): After Elijah's victory at Mount Carmel where 450 prophets of Baal are defeated, Jezebel threatens Elijah's life with an oath by her gods. Her power to terrorize even victorious Elijah reveals the depth of her influence. This death threat triggers Elijah's complete breakdown, his flight to Mount Horeb, and ultimately God's command to anoint his replacement‚Äîeffectively ending Elijah's active ministry.
Naboth's Vineyard (1 Kings 21): Jezebel orchestrates a judicial conspiracy using false witnesses to murder Naboth and steal his ancestral inheritance. This act crystallizes her contempt for Israelite law and covenant values, prompting Elijah's prophecy of doom.
Defiant Downfall (2 Kings 9:30‚Äì37): Facing Jehu with painted eyes and adorned head, Jezebel maintains regal defiance to the end. Thrown from the palace window, trampled by horses, and devoured by dogs, her death precisely fulfills Elijah's prophecy.
Pattern Recognition: Jezebel functions as the anti-Queen of Sheba‚Äîwhere one foreign queen blessed Yahweh and brought gifts to honor Israel's God, Jezebel brings curses and destroys covenant loyalty. Her narrative mirrors the "strange woman" of Proverbs who leads to death. Sweeney notes she represents the ultimate threat of Canaanization. Significantly, her single threat accomplishes what 450 defeated prophets could not‚Äîbreaking Elijah's resolve and precipitating his ministry's end.
