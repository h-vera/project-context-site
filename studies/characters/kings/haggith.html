Summary: Haggith appears in Scripture primarily as the mother of Adonijah, David's fourth son born in Hebron. Her son's attempt to seize the throne leverages his position as a senior surviving son, and the narrative repeatedly identifies him through his maternal lineage as "Adonijah son of Haggith."

Theological Significance: Haggith represents how maternal lineage played into royal legitimacy claims, yet her son's failure demonstrates that God's election surpasses human custom and birthright. Divine choice, not maternal pedigree, determines succession in God's kingdom.

Narrative Journey

Royal Birth Registry (2 Samuel 3:4): Listed among David's wives in Hebron, Haggith bears his fourth son, Adonijah. The genealogical record establishes her place in the royal household and her son's seniority among David's children.
Son's Self-Exaltation (1 Kings 1:5-6): Adonijah proclaims "I will be king!" and prepares chariots, horsemen, and fifty men to run before him. The text notes David never disciplined him, and emphasizes his handsome appearance‚Äîechoing Absalom's failed coup.
Maternal Identity Emphasized (1 Kings 1:11): Nathan warns Bathsheba about "Adonijah son of Haggith" becoming king. The repeated maternal identifier underscores how mothers' identities factored into succession disputes and political legitimacy.
Failed Succession (1 Kings 1:13-53): Despite Adonijah's seniority and supporters, Solomon is anointed king through prophetic intervention and divine choice, demonstrating that God's election transcends birth order.
Pattern Recognition: Lineage provides claim but not guarantee. The narrative shows that while maternal identity matters in royal politics, covenant promise and divine election ultimately determine leadership in Israel.
