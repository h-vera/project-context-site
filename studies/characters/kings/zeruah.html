Summary: Zeruah, identified as a widow from Ephraim, was the mother of Jeroboam I who rebelled against Solomon and became the first king of the Northern Kingdom. Her name, meaning "stricken" or "leprous," may suggest stigma or suffering, highlighting the humble origins of Israel's divisive leader.

Theological Significance: By naming Jeroboam's widowed mother, the text personalizes the kingdom's fracture and roots it in ordinary family history. Her name potentially encodes the "stricken" nature of the division itself, showing how God can raise leaders from stigmatized backgrounds to accomplish His sovereign purposes.

Narrative Journey

Sole Biblical Mention (1 Kings 11:26): "Jeroboam son of Nebat, an Ephraimite of Zeredah, whose mother's name was Zeruah, a widow, was Solomon's servant but rebelled against the king." This single verse contains her entire biblical presence, yet it carries significant weight in establishing Jeroboam's identity.
Widowhood and Social Position: Her identification as a widow suggests vulnerability and lower social status. In ancient Israel, widows depended on community support, making Jeroboam's rise from such origins to kingship even more remarkable.
Name as Commentary: The name "Zeruah" (stricken/leprous) may function as narrative commentary on the divided kingdom‚Äîwhat appears as political advancement carries the mark of affliction and separation from the covenant community.
Maternal Legacy: Though mentioned only once, her inclusion in Jeroboam's introduction anchors his identity. The narrator's choice to name her while omitting other details suggests her significance in understanding the human dimension of Israel's division.
Pattern Recognition: Naming mothers in royal genealogies often signals theological or political significance. Zeruah's name and status encode both the humble origins and the wounded nature of the northern kingdom's founding.
