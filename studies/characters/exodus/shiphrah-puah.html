Summary: Pharaoh commands the Hebrew midwives to kill infant boys at birth, but Shiphrah and Puah "fear God" and let the boys live. Their shrewd response to Pharaoh exposes his blindness about the power of daughters and inaugurates the Exodus narrative of life overcoming death through faithful women.

Theological Significance: Their fear of God over the king reframes true authority as service to life. These women's courageous action preserves God's covenant people and prepares the way for Israel's deliverance.

Narrative Journey

Pharaoh's Genocidal Edict (Ex 1:15-16): The king of Egypt commands Shiphrah and Puah to kill Hebrew boys at birth while letting the girls live (◊ó÷∏◊ô÷∏◊î - khayah). This decree represents an anti-creation assault on God's covenant promise.
Defiant Fear of God (Ex 1:17): The midwives "fear God" and let the boys live, choosing divine authority over royal command. Their civil disobedience becomes the first act of resistance in the Exodus narrative.
Shrewd Deception (Ex 1:18-19): When questioned, they tell Pharaoh that Hebrew women are ◊ó÷∏◊ô◊ï÷π◊™ (khayoth - "lively/vigorous"), giving birth before midwives arrive. Their wordplay uses "life" language to deceive the death-dealer.
Divine Blessing (Ex 1:20-21): God deals well with the midwives, blessing them with families of their own. Israel continues to multiply, fulfilling the creation mandate despite oppression.
Pattern Recognition: Women preserve life against a serpent-like tyrant who seeks to destroy God's people. Their courage becomes the seed of Israel's liberation, anticipating how God will use women throughout the Exodus story.

Ancient Near Eastern Context & Biblical Distinctives

üìú ANE Parallels

  • Royal Population Control: State-sponsored infanticide and birth suppression documented in ancient sources
  • Midwifery Practices: Professional midwives held significant social roles in Egypt and Mesopotamia
  • Divine vs. Royal Authority: Tension between divine and human commands common in ANE literature

‚ö° Biblical Distinctives

  • Valorization of Resistance: God honors civil disobedience that preserves life
  • Women's Agency: Women as primary agents of redemption, not mere victims
  • Divine Blessing: God's favor tied to protection of the vulnerable, not state power

Messianic Trajectory & New Testament Connections

Life-Preserving Women: Anticipate the gospel's upside-down kingdom where power serves the weak and the marginalized become agents of salvation.
Pattern of Deliverance: God working through faithful women to preserve life establishes a pattern fulfilled in Mary's role in bringing forth the Messiah.
Civil Courage: Their example of obeying God rather than human authorities prefigures apostolic resistance: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29).

Application & Reflection

Personal

  • Practice courageous truth-telling that protects the vulnerable
  • Choose to fear God above earthly consequences
  • Use wisdom and shrewdness in confronting injustice

Community

  • Honor and platform women's agency in God's mission
  • Resist dehumanizing systems through collective action
  • Create networks of protection for the vulnerable
Contemporary Challenge: When civil authorities demand participation in harm, the fear of God must override the fear of human power. True faith acts to preserve life even at personal cost.

Study Questions

  1. How does "fearing God" reshape our understanding of civil courage and resistance to unjust authority?
  2. What does it mean that Exodus begins by centering women as primary agents of deliverance?
  3. How does the wordplay on "life" (khayah) throughout this passage shape its theological message?
  4. What "life vs. death" conflicts exist in our world today, and what would faithful midwifery look like?
  5. How do Shiphrah and Puah's actions point forward to Christ's victory over death?
  6. What does this story teach about God's use of the marginalized to accomplish his purposes?
  7. How might this narrative have encouraged the original Israelite audience in Egypt or exile?
  8. What aspects of this story challenge modern assumptions about power, authority, and resistance?
