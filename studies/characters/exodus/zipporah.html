Summary: Zipporah, daughter of Reuel/Jethro the Midianite priest, enters the Exodus narrative when Moses flees Egypt to Midian. She becomes his wife after he defends her and her sisters at the well. In a strange and critical scene on the journey back to Egypt, Yahweh seeks to put Moses to death, but Zipporah circumcises their son and touches Moses with the foreskin, averting divine judgment. Through this act, she saves Moses' life and ensures his mission can continue.

Theological Significance: Zipporah embodies the role of the outsider who secures covenant faithfulness. Her intervention in Exodus 4 is enigmatic but crucial: Moses, chosen as covenant mediator, cannot fulfill his mission while neglecting the covenant sign of circumcision. Zipporah steps in where Moses fails, becoming the agent who preserves God's plan of deliverance. She completes the chain of seven women in Exodus 1-4 who rescue Moses and ensure Israel's deliverance.

Narrative Journey

At the Well (Ex 2:15-22): Moses flees to Midian after killing an Egyptian, defends Reuel's daughters at the well, and is welcomed into their household. Reuel gives Zipporah in marriage to Moses. She bears him a son, Gershom ("I have been a stranger there"), embodying Moses' liminal identity between Egypt, Midian, and Israel.
The "Bridegroom of Blood" Incident (Ex 4:24-26): On the journey back to Egypt, God seeks to kill Moses‚Äîlikely because his son was uncircumcised. Zipporah quickly circumcises the boy and touches (or throws) the foreskin at Moses' feet, declaring: "Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me." Her decisive action averts divine wrath, saving Moses and enabling his mission.
Reunion at Sinai (Ex 18:2-6): Zipporah is later reunited with Moses when Jethro brings her and their children to meet him after the exodus from Egypt, signaling her continued importance within the covenant family.
Pattern Recognition: Zipporah's story follows a pattern of outsider women who preserve God's covenant purposes (Tamar, Rahab, Ruth). Her actions at critical moments ensure the continuity of God's redemptive plan through Moses.

Ancient Near Eastern Context & Biblical Distinctives

üìú ANE Parallels

  • Well Scenes: Common marriage/betrothal motif in ANE literature where meetings at wells signify divine providence
  • Ritual Blood: Blood rituals often sealed covenants and averted divine wrath in ANE practice
  • Foreign Wives: Intermarriage with neighboring peoples was common but often viewed negatively in ANE texts

‚ö° Biblical Distinctives

  • Positive Outsider: Unlike ANE stories where foreign wives threaten identity, Zipporah preserves covenant faithfulness
  • Female Agency: Zipporah acts decisively in religious matters typically reserved for males in ANE contexts
  • Covenant Over Ethnicity: Knowledge of and commitment to covenant supersedes ethnic boundaries

Application & Reflection

Personal

  • Outsider status does not disqualify one from God's purposes; faith and decisive action matter
  • Courageous obedience may require quick, difficult, even costly intervention
  • God can use anyone who understands and upholds his covenant ways

Community

  • Women's voices and actions are integral to covenant faithfulness
  • The covenant community must remember that God often works through outsiders
  • Cross-cultural marriages can strengthen rather than weaken covenant identity when grounded in shared faith
Contemporary Challenge: Zipporah's story challenges assumptions about who can be instruments of God's purposes. Her Midianite identity and female gender might have disqualified her in human eyes, yet she becomes the agent through whom God preserves his chosen deliverer.

Study Questions

  1. Why does Exodus highlight Zipporah's role rather than Moses' in the circumcision scene?
  2. How does Zipporah's action exemplify covenant fidelity when Moses, the chosen deliverer, fails?
  3. What does "bridegroom of blood" mean in its context, and how does it resonate with biblical theology of covenant?
  4. How does Zipporah's role complete the "seven women" arc of Exodus 1-4?
  5. What can we learn about God's sovereignty from his use of a Midianite woman to preserve his covenant?
  6. How does her outsider status enrich the theme of Gentile inclusion in God's plan?
  7. What parallels exist between Zipporah's blood ritual and the Passover?
  8. How might Zipporah's decisive faith challenge contemporary views on gender and religious authority?
