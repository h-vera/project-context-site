The Messianic Trajectory

Matthew 1:5-6: "Salmon the father of Boaz by Rahab, and Boaz the father of Obed by Ruth, and Obed the father of Jesse, and Jesse the father of David the king."

Ruth is one of only five women named in Jesus' genealogy:

  • Tamar ‚Äî Canaanite who preserved Judah's line
  • Rahab ‚Äî Canaanite prostitute who showed faith
  • Ruth ‚Äî Moabite who chose covenant loyalty
  • Bathsheba ‚Äî "Wife of Uriah" the Hittite
  • Mary ‚Äî Young virgin who bore the Messiah

All have "irregular" stories highlighting God's grace transcending human boundaries. The Moabite woman's faith grafts her into the messianic line, demonstrating that God's redemptive plan always included the nations.

Theological Significance: Ruth's inclusion proves that the Messiah comes not just for the Gentiles but through them. A woman from a cursed people (Deut 23:3) becomes the great-grandmother of Israel's greatest king and ancestor of humanity's Redeemer.

Ruth in the Biblical Canon: Theological Synthesis

üìú Looking Backward

Ruth demonstrates Torah fulfilled not through legalism but through ◊ó÷∂◊°÷∂◊ì‚Äîcovenant love that exceeds requirements. She embodies the Abrahamic faith pattern, showing that covenant membership was always about faith, not ethnicity.

üåü Present Context

Set in Judges' darkness ("everyone did what was right"), Ruth offers light‚Äîshowing true righteousness through ordinary faithfulness. Her story provides hope that God works even in the darkest periods through faithful individuals.

Canonical Significance: Ruth functions as Scripture's theological hinge‚Äîlooking back to Torah's vision of justice, sideways to Wisdom's portrait of excellence, and forward to the gospel's inclusion of all peoples.
