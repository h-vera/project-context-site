Chapter 1: Emptiness & Pledge

Primary Text: Ruth 1:1-22

Theme: From fullness to emptiness, from home to exile and back

"But Ruth said, 'Do not urge me to leave you or to return from following you. For where you go I will go, and where you lodge I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my God.'" Ruth 1:16

This highlights that Ruth's choice was neither culturally expected nor inevitable, but an extraordinary act of covenant faith. Chapter 2: Field & Favor

Primary Text: Ruth 2:1-23

Theme: Providence through "chance" and exceeding the law with grace

"May the LORD reward you for your deeds, and may you have a full reward from the LORD, the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge." Ruth 2:12

This deliberate parallel shows Boaz recognizing Ruth's Abrahamic-level faith, establishing her as a true covenant member despite her Moabite origins. He sees beyond ethnicity to covenant faith.

Discussion Questions from Character Insights

Q1

How does Orpah's reasonable choice illuminate Ruth's radical faith?

Guide: Compare the two choices at the crossroads. Why doesn't the narrator condemn Orpah? What does this teach about the cost of covenant commitment?

Character Insight: Orpah represents the natural human response‚Äîchoosing visible security over invisible faith. Her story shows that proximity to God's people doesn't guarantee participation in God's purposes.
Q2

What does Boaz's recognition of Ruth's "Abrahamic faith" (2:11) reveal about belonging?

Guide: Explore the Genesis 12:1 parallel. How does this reshape our understanding of who belongs in God's covenant family?

Character Insight: Boaz sees beyond Ruth's Moabite identity to her covenant faith, demonstrating prophetic insight that God's promises always included the nations.
Q3

How does Naomi's strategic wisdom challenge passive faith?

Guide: Trace Naomi's movement from bitter lament to active participation. When is bold initiative an act of faith?

Character Insight: Naomi's plan shows faith taking action for restoration. Her transformation from "Mara" to joy demonstrates that God often works through human wisdom and initiative.
Q4

What does "So-and-so's" namelessness teach about the cost of self-protection?

Guide: Contrast the nearer kinsman's calculation with Boaz's generosity. How does fear of loss lead to greater loss?

Character Insight: The unnamed kinsman chose property over people, inheritance over inclusion. His erasure from history warns against prioritizing security over redemptive opportunity.
Q5

How does the "wings" motif reveal divine-human partnership?

Guide: Trace the journey from Boaz's prayer (2:12) to Ruth's request (3:9). What does this teach about being God's hands and feet?

Theological Insight: The ◊õ÷∏÷º◊†÷∏◊£ wordplay shows how divine protection works through human covenant faithfulness. We become the wings under which others find refuge.

Comprehensive Study Questions

These questions draw from our character profiles and theological analysis:

  2. What does Naomi's honest lament (calling herself Mara) teach about a healthy relationship with God even in suffering?
