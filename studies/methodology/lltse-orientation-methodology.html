Here’s the Why

I created the Literal–Literary Translation Structure Edition (LLTSE) because paragraph Bibles often muffle what Scripture is trying to make you hear. Hebrew breathes in parallel lines and mirrored thought; Greek picks up that rhythm in creeds, triads, and nested clauses. When those patterns are pressed into uniform paragraphs with bold headings and devotional sub‑titles, much of the logic and cadence disappears. I wanted a page where the structure teaches before any commentary does.

So this edition keeps translation literal where possible and literary where necessary, then lets the layout carry meaning: indentation for subordination, stacked lines for lists and triads, spacing for chiasm and inclusio. It removes overlays (titles, sub‑titles, intrusive verse numbers) from the reading flow and brings forward what the original audiences would have recognized immediately—audible balance and rhetorical design.

Project Context exists to help readers inhabit the world of the text with clarity. LLTSE is one of the site’s core tools for that: a way to display Hebrew and Greek thought—faithfully rendered in English—so the argument and the music of the Scriptures are visible at a glance.

What follows is the orientation to this method — the Literal–Literary Translation Structure Edition.

Methodology: Hebrew and Greek in Dialogue

In Scripture, language evolution is itself a theology of communication. Hebrew writers conveyed ideas through rhythm and repetition; Greek writers inherited those forms to express revelation across cultures. The LLTSE shows this continuity: how Hebrew poetry shaped Greek argumentation, and how both converge in English through structural translation.

Why Indentation Works

  • Visual hierarchy: reveals clauses and sub‑clauses at a glance.
  • Oral performance: natural pauses align with the line breaks.
  • Syntactic clarity: structure mirrors the grammar of the source.
  • Pedagogical value: aids memorization and commentary writing.
  • Cross‑cultural translation: bridges Hebrew thought and Greek expression into English rhythm.

Evolution & Rationale

LLTSE builds on centuries of observation about biblical structure:

  • Robert Lowth — Hebrew parallelism as the foundation of biblical poetry.
  • Robert Alter & James Kugel — how parallelism and “seconding” lines drive meaning.
  • Adele Berlin & F. W. Dobbs‑Allsopp — modern lineation: syntax and rhythm together.
  • Nils Lund & John Breck — chiastic and hymnic structures in the New Testament.

Principles of Translation & Design

Translation

  • Literal where possible; literary where necessary to preserve sense and cadence.
  • Destination chain: Hebrew → Aramaic → Greek → English; Greek often expresses Hebrew thought‑forms.
  • De‑layering: remove editorial overlays (titles, sub‑titles, devotional headers) from the reading flow.
  • Clause fidelity: indentation reflects subordination, not English stylistic preference.
  • Oral readability: line breaks follow natural pauses and thought‑units.

Layout & Typography

  • Lineation: each line is a semantic unit (cola/stichs; lists/triads set as vertical stacks).
  • Chiasm & Inclusio: mirrored lines and bookends surfaced via alignment and spacing.
  • Verse numbers: available in refs/hover—not in the main reading flow.
  • Consistency: Hebrew RTL and Greek/English LTR harmonized without forcing English word order.
  • Accessibility: screen‑reader friendly structure; responsive on mobile and large displays.

What LLTSE Is Not

  • Not a paraphrase: wording remains anchored to the source language; layout reveals structure without rewording sense units.
  • Not an interlinear: this is idiomatic English aligned to source syntax and discourse, not word-for-word glossing.
  • Not a study Bible: headings, sub‑titles, and notes are kept out of the reading flow; the layout itself does the first level of teaching.
