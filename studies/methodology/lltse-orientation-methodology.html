The Scholarly Consensus

Modern biblical scholarship has long recognized that Hebrew poetry works through parallelism and Greek rhetoric through careful clause arrangement. LLTSE simply makes this visible on the page, following the practice of scholars like Robert Alter, Adele Berlin, and F.W. Dobbs-Allsopp who lineate biblical texts in their academic translations.

ğŸ“š Evolution & Rationale

LLTSE builds on centuries of scholarly observation about biblical structure. The foundations were laid in the 18th century and continue to develop today:

Robert Lowth (1753): First to systematically identify Hebrew parallelism as the defining feature of biblical poetry. His Lectures on the Sacred Poetry of the Hebrews established categories: synonymous, antithetical, and synthetic parallelism.
Robert Alter (1985, 2011): The Art of Biblical Poetry showed how parallelism works through intensification and specificationâ€”second lines don't merely repeat but advance the thought. His own translations demonstrate structural lineation.
James Kugel (1981): The Idea of Biblical Poetry emphasized the "seconding" functionâ€”"There is A, and what's more there is B." Challenged simple equation of parallel lines while highlighting their forward movement.
Adele Berlin (1985): The Dynamics of Biblical Parallelism provided detailed linguistic analysis of how parallelism operates at multiple levels (grammatical, lexical, semantic, phonological).
F.W. Dobbs-Allsopp (2015): On Biblical Poetry demonstrated that biblical poetry is free verse, not metricalâ€”line structure is determined by semantic units and natural pauses, justifying flexible lineation.
Nils Lund (1942) & John Breck (1994): Documented extensive chiastic and hymnic structures in the New Testament, showing how Greek writers used Hebrew literary patterns to express theological truth.
LLTSE's Contribution: We apply these established scholarly principles more comprehensively and systematically to both testaments, creating a reading edition that makes structure visible without imposing meter or arbitrary formatting.

Translation Philosophy

  • Literal where possible; literary where necessary to preserve sense and cadence.
  • Destination chain awareness: Hebrew â†’ Aramaic â†’ Greek â†’ English; Greek often expresses Hebrew thoughtâ€‘forms.
  • Deâ€‘layering: Remove editorial overlays (titles, subâ€‘titles, devotional headers) from the reading flow.
  • Clause fidelity: Indentation reflects source-language subordination, not English stylistic preference.
  • Oral readability: Line breaks follow natural pauses and thoughtâ€‘units that ancient audiences would have heard.
  • Semantic units: Each line represents a complete thought or clause (cola/stichs in Hebrew; sense units in Greek).

Layout & Typography

  • Lineation: Each line is a semantic unit; lists and triads set as vertical stacks for clarity.
  • Chiasm & Inclusio visibility: Mirrored lines and bookends surfaced via alignment and spacing.
  • Verse numbers: Available in references/hoverâ€”not embedded in the main reading flow to avoid interrupting rhythm.
  • Directional integrity: Hebrew RTL and Greek/English LTR harmonized without forcing English word order to match source.
  • Accessibility: Screenâ€‘reader friendly structure; responsive design for mobile and large displays.
  • Typography hierarchy: Uses indentation, not bold/italic overload, to show relationships.

Balance in Practice

LLTSE navigates the tension between formal equivalence (word-for-word) and dynamic equivalence (thought-for-thought). We preserve source structure and syntax where English allows, but prioritize clarity and natural expression where literal rendering would obscure meaning. The goal: let English readers see what Hebrew and Greek readers saw in the original literary architecture.
