9th Grade Literature · Study Guide & Exam

Night

Elie Wiesel — Chapter 5

I Chapter 5 — Study Reference
Key Points Summary

What Happens in Chapter 5

Chapter 5 is the emotional and spiritual turning point of the entire memoir. Set during the Jewish High Holidays at Buna Labor Camp (Auschwitz III), it is where Elie's faith finally breaks — not into atheism, but into open accusation against God.

Setting · Time
Autumn 1944 — Buna Labor Camp
Chapter 5 spans Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — roughly a year after Elie's arrival. Buna (Auschwitz III / Monowitz) is where Elie works sorting electrical parts.
Faith Crisis
Rosh Hashanah: Elie Refuses to Pray
Ten thousand prisoners gather to bless God. Elie cannot. He feels rage rather than gratitude — he stands as an accuser, not a worshiper. This is the climax of his spiritual rupture.
Yom Kippur · Defiance
Elie Chooses Not to Fast
On the Day of Atonement, Elie deliberately eats. He will not add to his own suffering to honor a God he believes has abandoned him. His father advises fasting for safety — Elie refuses.
Survival · Fear
The Selection — Shlomo's Near Death
Dr. Mengele conducts a selection. Shlomo is initially sent to the condemned side but crosses back in the confusion. Elie runs alongside him to appear strong. Relief is shadowed by immediate guilt.
Loss of Community
Akiba Drumer Is Selected
Devout Akiba, who had sustained others through song, loses faith and then the will to live. He is selected. He asks friends to recite Kaddish for him after his death — they forget.
Physical Suffering
Elie's Foot Surgery
Elie's foot becomes dangerously infected. He is admitted to the camp infirmary for surgery. Doctors say he will recover in two weeks — but an evacuation order will soon force him to march.
"

Why, but why should I bless Him? In every fiber I rebelled. Because He had had thousands of children burned in His pits? Because He kept six crematories working night and day, on Sundays and feast days? Because in His great might He had created Auschwitz, Birkenau, Buna, and so many factories of death?

— Elie Wiesel, Night · Chapter 5

Major Themes in Chapter 5
Faith & Doubt
Elie's rebellion against God is the emotional climax — not disbelief, but anguished accusation. God is put on trial.
Dehumanization
The selection reduces life to a glance. Akiba's forgotten Kaddish shows how even mourning rituals erode under extreme suffering.
Father & Son
Elie and Shlomo's bond is tested by the selection. Survival increasingly pulls individuals toward solitary self-preservation.
Night as Symbol
Darkness becomes total. The fires of the crematorium mirror the extinguished flames of Elie's faith.
II Big Idea Questions Whole Book · Discussion

These questions span the entire memoir. Think carefully — there are no single right answers, only thoughtful or unthoughtful ones.

1
What does it mean to remain human under dehumanizing conditions?

What moments in Night show people clinging to humanity — and what moments show it stripped away? What does Wiesel suggest is the cost of survival?

✦ Discussion Guide
Look for: moments of humanity vs. moments of its loss
Scenes showing humanity: Elie running alongside his father during the selection; the French girl who consoles Elie after Idek's beating; prisoners sharing rations. Scenes showing its loss: Rabbi Eliahou's son running ahead on the death march; men fighting over bread and killing their own fathers; Elie's silence as his father is beaten. Wiesel does not moralize — he observes. His point is that dehumanization is not a character flaw but a designed system. The camps engineered prisoners to be complicit in their own degradation. The cost of survival was often a piece of one's humanity.
2
Is Night ultimately a story about the death of faith — or the transformation of faith?

Elie begins deeply religious and ends unable to pray. But is his anger toward God a form of relationship with God? Does the memoir leave room for any kind of faith at the end?

✦ Discussion Guide
Key tension: accusation presupposes the existence of someone to accuse
The most thoughtful responses note that Elie never simply stops believing — he rails against God, which presupposes God's existence. The Jewish tradition has precedent for arguing with God: Abraham over Sodom, Job demanding an answer, Moses interceding for Israel. Wiesel himself continued to wrestle with God theologically throughout his life. The memoir ends without restoration — Elie sees a corpse in the mirror. But the act of writing Night is itself an act of faith: bearing witness implies truth matters, memory matters, and the dead deserve to be remembered. That is a form of devotion even without prayer.
3
Why does silence — of nations, of God, of bystanders — matter so much in Night?

"The opposite of love is not hate — it is indifference." How does indifference appear in Night? Think about the world's failure to act, God's silence on Rosh Hashanah, and the townspeople who ignored Moshe the Beadle.

✦ Discussion Guide
Three levels of silence — each one enabled the next stage of atrocity
Community silence: The people of Sighet refused to believe Moshe's warnings — enabling denial that allowed deportation. World silence: Allied governments who knew about the camps and did not act — enabling their continued operation. God's silence: Elie's Rosh Hashanah crisis — representing the ultimate abandonment. To read Night is to receive the testimony Moshe could not get anyone to hear. The question Wiesel poses to every reader: now that you know — will you be silent too?
4
How does Night function as an act of witness — and what responsibility does that place on the reader?

Wiesel survived when millions did not. He chose to write this memoir after years of silence. What does bearing witness mean, and what is the reader's responsibility after finishing this book?

✦ Discussion Guide
Bearing witness transforms private suffering into public memory — and obligation
Wiesel waited ten years before writing Night. His original Yiddish title — Un di velt hot geshvign ("And the World Remained Silent") — was itself an accusation. To bear witness is to refuse silence. It says: this happened, these people existed, they deserve to be remembered. Wiesel was explicit throughout his life that the lesson of the Holocaust is a standing obligation — resist indifference, speak out against injustice, remember. The strongest responses move beyond summary to honest personal reflection: what does this obligation mean in your own school, community, and world?

The dominant emotional register of Chapter 5 is a ringing spiritual emptiness. Elie stands surrounded by ten thousand praying men yet feels utterly alone. The opening passage uses fire as a symbol of destruction and permanent memory. In Chapter 5, fire reappears in the flames of the crematoria and in Elie's burning rage. The passage is written in past tense by the adult Wiesel, meaning the faith lost in Chapter 5 is never recovered in the memoir.

Kaddish is one of the most sacred obligations in Jewish life — to honor the dead. The message is that the camps did not only kill bodies — they eroded the capacity to be human. This is exactly what Wiesel wants readers to understand: genocide attacks not just bodies but the entire fabric of human dignity.
IV Fill in the Blank Key Terms & Answers

Each question below identifies a key term, name, or concept from Chapter 5. The answer and explanation are shown beneath each one.

01
During Rosh Hashanah, Elie feels [ blank ] toward God rather than gratitude, marking a turning point in his faith.
Answer
Rage / Rebellion / Anger
Wiesel describes himself as an accuser rather than a worshiper. Instead of the traditional posture of humility and gratitude, he feels burning rage at God for allowing Auschwitz to exist. This is not ordinary doubt — it is an active spiritual revolt.
02
The process by which SS officers divided prisoners into those who would live and those condemned to death was called a [ blank ].
Answer
Selection (Selektion)
The Selection (German: Selektion) was a regular feature of the camps. Officers — often Dr. Mengele at Birkenau — inspected prisoners and directed them left or right. Left typically meant immediate death; right meant continued forced labor. The process stripped any sense of justice or agency.
03
On Yom Kippur, Elie chose not to fast because he refused to [ blank ], seeing no reason to afflict himself for God.
Answer
"Add to his own suffering" / "honor a God who had abandoned him"
Yom Kippur fasting is an act of self-denial meant to demonstrate repentance and submission to God. Elie is already starving. Eating becomes both an act of defiance and of self-preservation.
04
Akiba Drumer, who once kept prisoners' spirits alive through [ blank ], lost his will to live after losing his faith.
Answer
Singing / Hasidic or Kabbalistic songs
Akiba sustained fellow prisoners through deeply spiritual religious songs. His music represented the continued presence of God even in the camp. When he stopped believing, he lost his reason to live — illustrating that inner conviction could be a survival mechanism, and its loss could prove fatal.
05
The Jewish mourning prayer Akiba asked his friends to recite after his death — which they forgot — is called [ blank ].
Answer
Kaddish
Kaddish is the Jewish memorial prayer recited by mourners. Ironically, it does not mention death at all — it is a praise of God's greatness. Akiba's request shows his final act of faith; the prisoners' forgetting shows how completely the camps had severed ties of community and religious practice.
06
Wiesel describes himself as standing apart, feeling like [ blank ] rather than a worshiper of God during the Rosh Hashanah service.
Answer
The accuser (with God as the accused)
One of the memoir's most famous lines: "I was the accuser, God the accused." Wiesel inverts the traditional relationship between human and divine. Rather than standing before God in humility, he puts God on trial — a posture with deep roots in Jewish tradition (Abraham, Job, Moses) but carrying unprecedented weight here.
07
Elie's infected [ blank ] required surgery; he recovered in the camp [ blank ].
Answer
Foot · Infirmary (hospital block)
Elie's foot becomes severely swollen and he is admitted to the camp infirmary. Doctors reassure him he will recover in two weeks — but word soon arrives that the camp is being evacuated. His choice to march rather than stay creates the harrowing death march narrative of Chapter 6.
08
Wiesel bitterly applies God's own creative power against Him, writing that God "in His great might had [ blank ] Auschwitz, Birkenau, Buna…"
Answer
Created
Wiesel deliberately uses the word "created" — echoing Genesis. God who creates the universe also, by inaction, "creates" the death camps. This is not atheism — it is the most painful form of theism: belief in a God who could have acted and chose not to.
09
After the selection, Elie's father tells him: "[ blank ]" — and Elie's brief relief is immediately shadowed by guilt.
Answer
"I passed."
Shlomo's two words carry enormous weight. Elie's guilt comes from the awareness that his love for his father has become entangled with fear and self-preservation. The camps had twisted even love into a calculation about survival — a moral tension Elie carries through the rest of the memoir.
10
The labor camp where Chapter 5 takes place — where Elie works in the electrical parts warehouse — is called [ blank ].
Answer
Buna (Auschwitz III / Monowitz)
Buna (officially Auschwitz III–Monowitz) was a forced labor camp built to supply the I.G. Farben synthetic rubber plant. It is the setting for the hanging of the pipel, the Rosh Hashanah service, Akiba's selection, and Elie's foot surgery — the heart of the memoir.
