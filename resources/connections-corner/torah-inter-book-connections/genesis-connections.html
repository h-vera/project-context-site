Every thread Genesis sends forward — creation language, Eden typology, covenant promises, royal priesthood, and the image of God traced through Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.

How to Read This Page

Each section below contains one book-pair (Genesis → Exodus, Genesis → Leviticus, etc.). Click a section header to expand it. Connections are listed with the Genesis reference on the left, the target-book reference on the right, and a description of the verbal, thematic, or structural link between them. Hebrew keywords are shown in italics where relevant.

For the intra-chapter connections within Genesis 1–2 that anchor many of these inter-book threads, see the dedicated Genesis 1–2 Connections page.

Genesis → Exodus
14 connections
▸
Genesis sets the stage; Exodus replays creation on a national scale. Israel's exodus recapitulates the creation narrative — spirit over waters, dry land appearing, a people "fruitful and multiplying" — and culminates in a sanctuary that mirrors Eden's architecture. The tabernacle is Genesis 1–2 in miniature.

Gen Lev
Genesis → Leviticus
12 connections
▸
Leviticus answers the question Genesis raises: on what basis can a holy God dwell among a sinful people? The Fall creates the problem; the sacrificial system, priestly mediation, holiness code, and Day of Atonement together provide the answer. Remove Leviticus and the Torah collapses — remove Genesis and Leviticus has no reason to exist.

Gen Deut
Genesis → Deuteronomy
16 connections
▸
The densest pair in the Torah. Deuteronomy is Genesis seen from the other side of 400 years — every promise given to the patriarchs is rehearsed, the covenant is renewed, the land is visible on the horizon. Genesis opens the story; Deuteronomy brings it to its first great climax. They are the A and A′ of the Torah's chiastic structure.
