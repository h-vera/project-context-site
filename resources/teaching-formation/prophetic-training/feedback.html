Feedback Culture

A safe, repeatable method for giving and receiving feedback. Formation, not evaluation. Always kind, always specific, always aimed at growth.

üí° Core Principle

Feedback in this team is never evaluative‚Äîit's formative. We're not grading sermons or ranking speakers. We're helping one another grow in faithfulness, clarity, and Christ-centeredness.

Feedback IS

  • A spiritual practice of love
  • Specific and actionable
  • Rooted in Scripture and theology
  • Given with humility and kindness
  • Oriented toward the speaker's growth

Feedback IS NOT

  • A performance review
  • A platform to show off knowledge
  • Vague or general comments
  • Given with harshness or condescension
  • About personal preference or style
Remember: "Let your conversation be always full of grace, seasoned with salt" (Colossians 4:6). Feedback must build up, never tear down.

ü´∂ The Affirm ‚Üí Clarify ‚Üí Form Method

Our feedback follows a three-step pattern, used both before and after practice:

1 Affirm

Begin by naming what's working. Where was Jesus clear? Where did Scripture come alive? Where did you sense the Spirit's presence?

Why this matters: Affirmation builds confidence and shows the speaker what to keep doing.

Example: "Your Big Idea was crystal clear‚ÄîI could repeat it right now. And the way you connected Jesus to the Exodus story was powerful. I felt the gospel in that moment."

2 Clarify

Ask one clarifying question to help the speaker sharpen their message. This isn't criticism‚Äîit's curiosity that serves their clarity.

Why this matters: Questions help speakers think more deeply without feeling attacked.

Example: "In movement two, you mentioned 'covenant faithfulness'‚Äîcould you say more about what that means for us today? I think I followed, but I wasn't 100% sure."

3 Form

Offer one specific, actionable growth invitation. Focus on content, not delivery. Frame it as an opportunity, not a deficit.

Why this matters: One clear next step is more helpful than ten vague suggestions.

Example: "Next time, I wonder if you could slow down at the Jesus moment‚Äîmaybe even pause for 3-5 seconds. That's the climax of the message, and it deserves space to breathe."

üõ°Ô∏è Feedback Guardrails

To keep feedback safe and formative, we commit to these cultural guardrails:

  • One person at a time ‚Äì No piling on; everyone gets space to speak
  • Affirmation always comes first ‚Äì Never lead with critique
  • One clarifying question maximum ‚Äì Don't overwhelm the speaker
  • One forming suggestion maximum ‚Äì Focus beats exhaustive lists
  • No "but" or "however" ‚Äì If you're affirming, affirm. Don't disguise critique.
  • Focus on content, not delivery ‚Äì We're forming theologians, not performers
  • Speaker has final say ‚Äì They can accept, adapt, or set aside feedback
If feedback doesn't feel like a gift, we're doing it wrong.

üéÅ How to Receive Feedback Well

Receiving feedback is a spiritual discipline. Here's how to do it humbly and wisely:

Listen Fully

Don't defend, explain, or interrupt. Just receive. You'll have time to process later.

Assume Love

Your team wants you to succeed. Even if feedback stings, trust their good intentions.

Say "Thank You"

Even if you disagree, gratitude honors the giver and keeps your heart teachable.

Test It Prayerfully

Not all feedback is equal. Bring it to the Lord. Ask: "Is this true? Is this helpful?"

Act on What Resonates

If three people say the same thing, pay attention. The Spirit often speaks through patterns.

"Whoever heeds life-giving correction will be at home among the wise." ‚Äî Proverbs 15:31
