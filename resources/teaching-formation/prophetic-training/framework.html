üß©The Framework (Acts-Shaped)

This is the repeatable logic of faithful proclamation found in every Acts sermon: rooted in text, moving toward Jesus, calling for response.

1) Text Unit

Choose a coherent passage (same letter/book) that can be read and traced without hopping around.

2) Context

What's happening in the book? Who is speaking? What is at stake?

3) Big Idea

One clear claim that arises from the passage and can be repeated by the congregation.

4) Tension / Question

Name the real issue the passage confronts (belief, repentance, fear, resistance, mission).

5) Movement / Structure

Show progression (Acts speeches often move as story argument, not just points).

6) Jesus Center

Acts consistently bears witness to Jesus: death, resurrection, exaltation, reign.

7) Edification

Strengthen / encourage / console. "What is the Spirit building in this church?"

8) Response

Concrete repentance/obedience + actionable next steps.

Acts rule of thumb: Proclamation is not "content delivery." It is witness that calls for response.

Example 1: Expository ‚Äì Peter at Pentecost (Acts 2:14-41)

üìñ Expository üéØ Evangelistic üë• Mixed Audience (Jews from every nation)

üìç Context

Day of Pentecost; Spirit poured out with tongues of fire; crowd amazed and confused hearing their own languages. Peter stands with the Eleven to address the gathered multitude from every nation.

üí° Big Idea

Jesus, whom you crucified, is risen Lord and Messiah who pours out the promised Spirit‚Äîrepent and be baptized to receive forgiveness and the gift of the Spirit.

‚ö° Tension

"What does this Spirit outpouring mean? Who is this Jesus we crucified? What should we do about it?"

üß© Structure (Text ‚Üí Fulfillment ‚Üí Witness ‚Üí Response)

Movement 1 (v.14-21): "This is what was spoken by the prophet Joel"‚Äîthe last days have arrived, God is pouring out His Spirit on all flesh, everyone who calls on the Lord will be saved.

Movement 2 (v.22-32): Jesus of Nazareth‚Äîattested by God through mighty works, handed over by God's plan, crucified by lawless men, BUT God raised Him because death could not hold Him. David prophesied the Messiah wouldn't see decay (Psalm 16)‚Äîthat's Jesus.

Movement 3 (v.33-36): Exalted to God's right hand, Jesus received the promised Spirit and has poured it out (what you see and hear). David called Him "Lord" (Psalm 110)‚ÄîGod has made this Jesus both Lord and Messiah.

Movement 4 (v.37-41): Cut to the heart: "What shall we do?" Peter: "Repent and be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ for forgiveness‚Äîreceive the gift of the Spirit. The promise is for you, your children, and all who are far off." 3,000 baptized that day.

‚úùÔ∏è Jesus Center

Jesus is the exalted Lord at God's right hand who pours out the Spirit. He is David's descendant who conquered death (Psalm 16 fulfilled), the Messiah whom God raised and seated at His right hand (Psalm 110 fulfilled). The Spirit's outpouring is proof of Jesus' lordship and messiahship.

‚Ü™Ô∏è Response

Repent (turn from resistance) and be baptized in Jesus' name. Receive forgiveness of sins and the gift of the Holy Spirit. The promise extends to you, your children, and everyone God calls.

Why this works: Peter uses OT texts his audience knows (Joel, David's Psalms), shows Jesus as fulfillment, proves it with resurrection, and calls for immediate response. Classic expository pattern: Scripture ‚Üí Jesus ‚Üí Response.

Example 2: Narrative ‚Äì Stephen's Defense (Acts 7:2-53)

üìö Narrative ‚öñÔ∏è Prophetic Confrontation üë• Hostile Audience (Sanhedrin)

üìç Context

Stephen arrested on false charges of blasphemy against Moses, Law, and Temple. Brought before Sanhedrin. His face shines like an angel. High priest asks "Are these charges true?" Stephen responds with epic narrative.

üí° Big Idea

Israel has a long pattern of resisting God's chosen messengers‚Äîand you're repeating it by rejecting Jesus, the Righteous One the prophets foretold.

‚ö° Tension

"Who is on trial here? Are we faithfully defending Moses and the Temple, or are we repeating Israel's pattern of resistance?"

üìö Story Panels (Pattern Exposition)

Panel 1 - Abraham: God appeared in Mesopotamia before the promised land‚ÄîGod's presence isn't tied to geography.

Panel 2 - Joseph: Rejected by his brothers, sold into slavery, BUT later God used him to save them‚Äîthey didn't recognize God's deliverer the first time.

Panel 3 - Moses (First Rejection): Raised in Egypt, tries to help his people, they reject him: "Who made you ruler and judge?"‚ÄîGod's deliverer refused.

Panel 4 - Moses (Second Time): God sends Moses back with signs and wonders, but Israel rejects him again in the wilderness, makes golden calf while receiving the Law‚Äîpattern continues.

Panel 5 - Temple: David found favor, Solomon built temple, BUT the Most High doesn't dwell in houses made by human hands (Isaiah 66)‚Äîyou've misunderstood what the Temple means.

Panel 6 - Prophets & Jesus: Your ancestors killed the prophets who foretold the Righteous One‚Äîand you have now betrayed and murdered Him. You received the Law but haven't kept it.

üîç Pattern Exposed

Israel repeatedly resists God's chosen messengers. The pattern: God sends ‚Üí people reject ‚Üí God persists ‚Üí pattern continues. Climax: murdering the Righteous One (Jesus) is the ultimate repetition of this pattern.

‚úùÔ∏è Jesus Center

Jesus is the Righteous One the prophets foretold‚Äîbetrayed and murdered just as ancestors killed the prophets. He is the greater Moses (rejected then vindicated), the greater Joseph (rejected then became savior). Stephen sees Him standing at God's right hand as advocate for His persecuted people.

‚Ü™Ô∏è Response (Implicit)

Stop resisting the Holy Spirit. Recognize the pattern. Repent of murdering the Messiah. (Stephen doesn't get to finish‚Äîthey stone him‚Äîbut the call is clear.)

Why this works: Stephen doesn't defend himself‚Äîhe prosecutes them. Uses narrative as indictment. Shows Israel's entire history is resistance to God's messengers, culminating in Jesus' murder. Brilliant inversion: They accuse Stephen of speaking against Moses and Temple; Stephen shows THEY are the ones who rejected Moses and misunderstood the Temple.

Example 3: Testimony ‚Äì Paul's Story in Jerusalem (Acts 22:1-21)

üí¨ Testimony üéØ Personal Witness üë• Hostile Jewish Crowd

üìç Context

Paul arrested after riot at temple. Roman commander about to flog him when Paul reveals Roman citizenship. Allowed to address crowd from steps. Speaks in Aramaic to get their attention.

üí° Big Idea

Jesus is the risen Lord who transforms His enemies into witnesses‚Äîmy life is proof.

‚ö° Tension

"How can this man who betrayed our traditions be trusted? Is his encounter with Jesus real or deception?"

üí¨ Testimony Structure (Before ‚Üí Encounter ‚Üí Now)

BEFORE (v.3-5): "I am a Jew, born in Tarsus but raised in this city, trained under Gamaliel in strict observance of the law, zealous for God just like all of you. I persecuted followers of the Way to the death, arrested and imprisoned men and women, had letters from the high priest to Damascus to arrest believers."

ENCOUNTER (v.6-16): "On the road to Damascus about noon, a brilliant light from heaven flashed around me. I fell to the ground and heard a voice: 'Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?' I asked 'Who are you Lord?' The voice said 'I am Jesus of Nazareth whom you are persecuting.' My companions saw the light but didn't understand the voice. 'What shall I do Lord?' He told me to go to Damascus where I'd be told what to do. The light had blinded me‚Äîmy companions led me by the hand into the city. Ananias came (a devout observer of the law, highly respected by all Jews living there)‚Äîhe restored my sight and said: 'The God of our ancestors has chosen you to know His will, to see the Righteous One and hear words from His mouth. You will be His witness to all people of what you have seen and heard.' I was baptized, calling on His name."

NOW (v.17-21): "Later, back in Jerusalem praying in the temple, I fell into a trance. Jesus spoke: 'Leave Jerusalem immediately because they will not accept your testimony about me.' I protested‚Äî'Lord, they know I imprisoned and beat believers in every synagogue, and when Stephen was killed I stood there giving approval.' But Jesus said: 'Go, I will send you far away to the Gentiles.'"

‚úùÔ∏è Jesus Center

Jesus is the risen Lord who appeared to Paul‚Äîpersonal encounter proves the resurrection. Jesus identifies with His persecuted church ("Why persecute ME?"). Paul's entire transformation from persecutor to apostle is evidence that Jesus lives and reigns. The Righteous One whom God chose Paul to see and hear‚Äîthe One worth losing everything for.

‚Ü™Ô∏è Response (Implicit)

Believe Paul's testimony. Jesus is alive. He transforms enemies. The mission to Gentiles comes from Jesus Himself. (Crowd listens peacefully until Paul mentions "Gentiles"‚Äîthen riots. Shows deep ethnic prejudice.)

Why this works: Paul adapts his testimony for Jewish audience‚Äîemphasizes his Jewish credentials, Ananias as "devout observer of the law highly respected by Jews," praying in the temple when commissioned. Same story as Acts 9 but tailored to audience. Testimony's power: "I was there, this happened to me, Jesus is alive." Keep Jesus as the hero, not yourself.

üóÇÔ∏èSermon Types (Acts-Based)

Types don't replace the framework‚Äîthey change how the framework is expressed. Each type still follows: text ‚Üí context ‚Üí Jesus ‚Üí response.

üìñ Expository (Text-driven walkthrough ‚Äî Acts 2 vibe) ‚ñæ
  • When: Clarity is needed; the text itself carries the message
  • How: Move through the passage in clean beats; keep anchors visible
  • Watch for: Over-explaining; losing the "so what" and response
  • Acts Examples: Peter at Pentecost (Acts 2), Peter after healing (Acts 3), Paul at Areopagus (Acts 17)

Map: Text ‚Üí Context ‚Üí Beat progression ‚Üí Jesus ‚Üí Response ‚Üí Edification

üìö Narrative (Story argument ‚Äî Acts 7 / Stephen) ‚ñæ
  • When: The audience needs reframing; history exposes a present pattern
  • How: Story panels ‚Üí pattern ‚Üí confrontation ‚Üí Jesus-centered clarity
  • Watch for: Info-dumping; missing the turn where pattern becomes "about us"
  • Acts Examples: Stephen's defense (Acts 7), Paul in Antioch (Acts 13)

Stephen's move: History is not trivia‚Äîit's a mirror showing our pattern

üí¨ Testimony (Witness ‚Äî Acts 22 / 26) ‚ñæ
  • When: You need credibility, relatability, and a clear gospel invitation
  • How: Before ‚Üí encounter ‚Üí now; tether every claim to Jesus
  • Watch for: Becoming the hero; the testimony must spotlight Christ
  • Acts Examples: Paul in Jerusalem (Acts 22), Paul before Agrippa (Acts 26)

Key principle: Jesus is the primary actor; you are bearing witness

üî• Prophetic Exhortation (Build-up ‚Äî 1 Cor 14:3 guardrails) ‚ñæ
  • When: The church needs strengthening, encouragement, consolation
  • How: Scripture-grounded + specific edification + discernment-friendly
  • Watch for: Vagueness; manipulation; shutting down testing with "God told me"
  • Acts Examples: Paul to Ephesian elders (Acts 20)

Always maintain: Order, humility, accountability, and testability
